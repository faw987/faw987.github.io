User Guide — JusticeMap-style Census Explorer

What this is

A small web page that draws a US county on a map and colors each census tract by a demographic or economic indicator you choose. It runs entirely from a single local HTML file and talks directly to the US Census Bureau.

Getting started

  1. Double-click justice_style_map_local.html. It opens in your default browser.
  2. You'll see a control panel in the upper left and a map of Manhattan, NY as the default view.
  3. Click Load map to fetch and draw data.

No installation, no accounts, no API keys needed for occasional use.

The controls

State FIPS (2 digits)

The Census Bureau's numeric code for a state. Examples: 06 California, 36 New York, 48 Texas, 17 Illinois.

County FIPS (3 digits)

The numeric code for a county within the chosen state. Always 3 digits — pad with leading zeros. In New York state: 061 Manhattan, 047 Brooklyn, 081 Queens.

ACS year

The vintage of the American Community Survey 5-year estimates used for income and poverty. This selector has no effect on race/ethnicity metrics, which come from the 2020 decennial census.

Metric

  • Median household income — dollar amount per tract (ACS table B19013).
  • Poverty rate — share of people with incomes below the poverty line (ACS table B17001).
  • Percent Black alone — share identifying as Black or African American alone (2020 PL, table P1).
  • Percent Hispanic/Latino — share of any race identifying as Hispanic or Latino (2020 PL, table P2).
  • Percent nonwhite — 100% minus the share identifying as non-Hispanic white alone. Hispanic residents of any race are counted as nonwhite.

Basemap

Background reference tiles. OpenStreetMap is denser; Carto Light is quieter and often reads better under a choropleth.

Reading the map

  • Each colored polygon is a census tract, roughly 1,200 to 8,000 residents.
  • Darker shades indicate higher values of the chosen metric. The legend in the lower right spells out the exact range for each color.
  • Gray tracts have missing or suppressed data.
  • Hover a tract to highlight its edge. Click a tract to see its name, the place/county/state hierarchy, the current metric value, and its GEOID.

Click anywhere to jump to that county

You don't need to look up FIPS codes. Click any point on the basemap (outside the currently loaded tracts) and the app will ask the Census which county contains that point, fill in the FIPS boxes for you, and reload the map. Clicks inside a tract still open that tract's popup.

The hamburger menu

  • User Guide — opens this document.
  • Panel size — switch the control panel between Small, Medium, and Large to reclaim screen space. Your choice is remembered between visits.

Sharing a view

  1. Pan and zoom until the map shows what you want.
  2. Click Copy shareable URL.
  3. Paste the link anywhere. Whoever opens it will see the same location, zoom, metric, and vintage.

Understanding the GEOID

A census tract GEOID is 11 digits: 2 for state, 3 for county, 6 for the tract itself (a 4-digit tract number plus a 2-digit suffix read as a decimal). So 36061010100 means New York state, New York County, tract 101.00. Left-to-right nesting, all FIPS codes.

If something goes wrong

  • "Please use a 2-digit state FIPS and 3-digit county FIPS." Check the format: California is 06, not 6.
  • "Load failed." Usually a typo in FIPS codes or a transient network problem.
  • Empty map with a legend. The county loaded but all tracts came back as missing data. Try a different metric or vintage.

Caveats

  • "Nonwhite" is a blunt instrument even with the non-Hispanic-white correction. It collapses very different communities into one residual category.
  • Tract boundaries change over time. Results for the same address in different years may not be directly comparable.
  • Small-area estimates have margins of error that are not shown here. Treat any single tract value as suggestive, not definitive.
